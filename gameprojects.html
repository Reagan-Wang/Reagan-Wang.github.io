Colour Coded - A One Button Game

Download Link: Colour Coded - A One Button Game
Team Members: Initial: Sarah Parks
Final: Sarah Parks, Grant Bortoli, Xinyun Shen, Matthias Horgen
Primary Role: Lead designer, producer
Project Time: First Prototype: 1 week
Final Production: 4 weeks

Colour Coded is a single-button game in which the player's objective is to move a ball to a goal using a variety of special platforms whose abilities are toggled on and off by the aforementioned single button.

This game is the result of taking one of my CSCI 426 prototypes (see below) and working on it a bit more with a larger team. I'm proud of how we managed to elaborate on the simple concept and work within the restrictions inherent in it.

Spell Showdown - An Information-Based Board Game

Rules
Team Members: Meilynn Poma, Suzy Jia, Scott Tang
Role: Lead Designer
Project Time 4 weeks

Spell Showdown was the final project of the class CTIN-488 (Games Workshop) at USC. Spell Showdown was a challenging game to design. The main mechanic around which the gameplay revolved was rather novel - something of a blend of Carcassonne and Code Names - which immediately posed a rule-learning problem for us to solve.

As lead designer, I was challenged to write a set of rules that could clearly and concisely the mechanics I wanted to convey - even recording a video explanation of the rules with the hypothesis that it would be a more natural way to teach the rules.

Success on this front was mixed - the concept inherently did not lend itself well to being picked up quickly, particularly by players inexperienced with board games. However, we did find improvements as we iterated on our instructions based off playtest feedback. The result was a game that, although tricky to learn, was interesting and compelling mechanically.

CSCI 426 Prototypes

As part of my coursework in CSCI426, Video Game Prototyping, I made a number of prototypes - to be specific, one prototype every week for seven weeks, working with only one partner. This inevitably meant that our prototypes were fairly rough and without much visual polish. Our priority was to explore potential design spaces as quickly as possible in order to find ideas that could be fleshed out.

Here are a few of my favourite prototypes made in this class.

Painting Pandemonium

Prompt: Choose a theme and design a game around it.
My partner and I decided to theme our game around painting, and so we designed our game around a painting mechanic, where players would try to cover the canvas with certain quantities of paint using their player character as a brush, a la Splatoon. Something we tried to take advantage of was the way this mechanic forces players to explore the entire canvas, but does not force them to do so in a linear manner - and so, a clever level designer could design levels with a number of different potential routes in mind.

Toggle

Prompt: Make a game using only one button.
Given our restriction to only one button, my partner and I thought of ways to get the most out of the spacebar. Our efforts led us to Toggle, in which the player toggles every single coloured platform on or off using their spacebar. The game was pretty easy programming-wise - most of our time here was spent designing engaging levels, as the game turned out to be a sort of physics-puzzler that at times required a keen sense of timing. This game ended up being chosen to be taken further, and my partner and I got to add two of our classmates to polish the game for a couple of weeks.

Shooty Spaceship Card Game...Thing

Prompt: Make a game using discrete input with continuous feedback
My latent addiction to Slay the Spire was on full display here - I proposed the concept to my partner with the reasoning "what could be more discrete than playing cards?". We decided that the game would work well if the player character was something that was ordinarily controlled indirectly - like a spaceship whose captain barked commands to their crew, creating a thematic justification for the one-at-a-time card controls. The prototype resulted in an intriguing potential design space which would marry deckbuilding elements with real-time strategy.
